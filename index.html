Interactive Data Visualisations — CPI & Global Cyclones

Domain and Purpose of Visualisation

The domain of this project focuses on cost of living and inflation trends in Australia, as reflected by the Consumer Price Index (CPI). Rising household expenses have broad implications for economic well-being, policy design, and affordability across Australian capital cities.

The dataset comes from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) — Consumer Price Index, Australia, June Quarter 2025 — which provides detailed CPI measures for both overall and category-specific expenditure groups across major cities.

This dashboard combines multiple visualisation types — bar, heatmap, radial, scatter, line, and geospatial — to highlight inflation dynamics, category drivers, and broader climate-economic contexts. The aim is to create an interactive narrative that supports exploration, comparison, and insight discovery.

How do CPI changes vary across Australia’s capital cities?

The bar chart highlights that Perth recorded the highest quarterly CPI increase of 1.9%, indicating stronger inflationary pressure than other cities. In contrast, Canberra and Darwin show minimal growth, reflecting more stable consumer prices. The difference in CPI across cities underlines the varied cost-of-living pressures Australians face based on geographic location.

Which categories contributed most to CPI change?

Across expenditure groups, Housing and Food & Non-Alcoholic Beverages dominate CPI increases, reflecting rising rents and grocery prices. Meanwhile, Clothing & Footwear also contributed notably with seasonal demand spikes. The analysis reveals that core living costs — shelter and essentials — are driving the overall inflation rate above 3.5% in several cities.

Which expenditure categories are most inflationary across most cities?

The heatmap reveals clear regional inflation clusters. Housing and Transport show consistently darker shades — signalling steep price increases across all capitals. Conversely, Communication and Education remain lighter, meaning stable or declining costs. The visual emphasises how housing and mobility remain the biggest contributors to nationwide CPI acceleration.

Which expenditure groups contributed the most to CPI change?

This radial chart visually communicates the proportional weight of each CPI group. Housing alone accounts for nearly 30% of total CPI variation, followed by Food and Transport. Together, these three categories shape the majority of price volatility in the Australian economy, underscoring how essential goods directly amplify consumer cost burdens.

How does Sydney’s CPI change compare to the national average?

The scatterplot compares Sydney’s CPI group changes against the weighted average of eight capital cities. For most categories, Sydney sits below the national average by 0.3–0.5%, indicating milder inflation pressure relative to peers. However, Housing and Health still show significant positive deviations, reflecting urban rent and service cost surges.

Which cities experienced the most extreme CPI changes in specific groups?

The parallel coordinates plot shows that Housing remains a volatile driver across all cities, especially in Perth and Brisbane, where price spikes exceed 2.0%. Health and Transport also fluctuate heavily between regions, suggesting localised supply chain impacts and differing state-level price policies.

Where in the world are tropical cyclones most frequent?

The cyclone map illustrates global storm density for the last 3 years. The Western Pacific and North Atlantic basins show the highest storm frequencies, with wind speeds surpassing 150 knots. These climatic disruptions highlight potential links between extreme weather events and regional inflation patterns, especially in sectors like transport and agriculture.

Conclusion

Overall, inflationary patterns remain uneven across Australia, with cities like Perth and Brisbane leading CPI growth while Canberra lags behind. Housing, Food, and Transport are confirmed as dominant inflation engines, aligning with national policy concerns. The combined view of economic and climatic data provides a holistic picture of how environmental and supply factors reinforce inflation risks.
